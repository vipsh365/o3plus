The Foundation: 3-Year Budget & Organizational Architecture

Before the 90-day breakdown, let me lay out the structural backbone that everything hangs on. This isn’t a 90-day activity. It’s a 2–3 year destination map. But the first 90 days is when we design it, validate it, and lock it in.

The starting point is a 3-year business plan built top-down and bottom-up simultaneously.

Top-down: we define revenue targets for Year 1, Year 2, Year 3, both top line and bottom line. Against that, we map the product range: which brands (O3+ Professional, DermaCult, Sara, Agelock, Biozoma, and any new labels), which categories within each brand, and the derived SKUs under each category. Every SKU gets a margin structure, not just gross margin, but channel-wise margin accounting for salon B2B pricing, modern trade margins, e-commerce commissions, general trade distributor margins, and export pricing.

Organizational Resource Planning

Once the revenue targets and vertical assignments are clear, we derive the complete resource requirement. From the 3-year budget, we cascade to department-level requirements. For each department (sales, marketing, brand management, R&D, supply chain, production, quality, finance, HR), we define what it needs to deliver. Then within each department, we break it into sub-departments or functional roles. For each role, we document four things:

  • JD (Job Description): specific to the business plan. The brand manager for DermaCult has a different JD than the brand manager for Sara, because the channels, price points, and consumer profiles are different.
  • KRA (Key Result Areas): 3–4 outcomes this role is accountable for, linked to departmental targets which are linked to the business plan.
  • KPI (Key Performance Indicators): measurable metrics tracked weekly and monthly. Revenue per territory for sales, formulation-to-approval cycle time for R&D, fill rate for supply chain.
  • Capability & background requirement: skills, experience level, and salary band this role demands.

This cascade gives us two things simultaneously. First, it tells us exactly how many people we need and where. Second, it tells us who in the current team fits, who needs training, and where we have gaps that require hiring.

Product Strategy: Master Products, Families & the Push-Pull Engine

For each sales segment, we identify or create a master product. The master product is the anchor. It’s the SKU that defines the brand’s presence in that segment. Differentiated enough to stand out, highly consumable enough to generate repeat purchase. It’s what the sales team leads with, what the marketing spend concentrates on, and what the consumer associates with the brand in that category. Think of it as the rocket: it goes up first, it’s visible, and everything else rides behind it.

Around each master product, we build a family of smaller complementary SKUs. These serve two purposes: they get pulled by the master product (a consumer buying the hero facial kit naturally moves to the serum, sunscreen, maintenance cream in the same range), and they push the master product from below (someone who starts with a smaller product and has a good experience gets pulled up to the full range). This is how you build ROI on both horizontal scale (more consumers across more touchpoints) and vertical scale (higher per-consumer spend through the product ladder).

It starts with brand management. Before any product goes to R&D, the brand manager delivers a complete brief: market survey findings, competition analysis (Indian and international benchmarks), target consumer profile, the specific USPs to inculcate in the formulation, the ingredient story, pricing architecture and margin structure at each channel level, and a clear articulation of why this product should exist.

R&D develops multiple formulations. These go through in-house testing, then consumer-facing evaluation (panel testing, salon trials, or controlled sampling). Formulations get shortlisted, pricing validated, packaging derived. Packaging should be designed for multiple-use potential wherever possible. If a bottle, tube, or jar spec can serve across products or brands, that’s procurement leverage and production simplification.

Once locked, the 360-degree marketing framework kicks in. Digital marketing (platform-specific), offline events, sales pitch and talking points, sample kits, collateral, and channel-specific volume projections. Modern trade shelf strategy, e-commerce listing optimization, salon introduction kits, export documentation: each planned separately. The sales team goes to each vertical with a clear brief: product, pitch, target, and how success gets measured.

FIFO & Inventory Aging Management

Every item in inventory, whether it’s an ingredient, a packaging component, or a finished product, gets tracked with entry date, batch number, and shelf life. FIFO is enforced at the warehouse level through system controls and physical layout design, not left to whoever’s picking stock that day.

Aging Band Status Action Protocol
0 – 6 months Fresh Normal sales flow, no intervention needed
6 – 12 months Aging Priority allocation to faster-moving channels, bundled into schemes or combo offers
12 – 24 months At Risk Active liquidation: cross-selling into adjacent channels, barter arrangements, discounted clearance through specific outlets (protecting brand perception in primary channels), reprocessing where ingredient stability allows
24+ months Dead Stock Evaluate for salvage value, write off where necessary, and root-cause analysis to fix the procurement/forecasting error that created it and prevent recurrence

The goal isn’t just to clear old stock. It’s to build a system where old stock stops accumulating because procurement is forecast-linked, production is demand-driven, and the MIS flags slow-moving items early enough to act.

Cost Efficiency Through Integrated Execution

The 20–25% efficiency improvement doesn’t come from a single lever. It comes from the system working together:

  • Formulation standardization: common bases across brands consolidate raw material procurement, reduce changeover time, and improve supplier rates on larger volumes.
  • Packaging rationalization: common formats wherever consumer-facing differentiation isn’t compromised. Fewer packaging SKUs, better vendor rates, less warehousing complexity.
  • Vendor consolidation: mapping overlaps across all labels and consolidating to fewer, larger-volume relationships.
  • Forecast-driven production: manufacturing runs based on demand forecasts validated against sales pipeline, not producing to stock “just in case.”
  • Inventory carrying cost reduction: FIFO enforcement, aging management, elimination of ad-hoc procurement.
  • Channel-specific margin optimization: understanding true cost-to-serve per vertical and adjusting product mix and pricing accordingly.

None of these are revolutionary individually. The leverage is in implementing them as a connected system.
